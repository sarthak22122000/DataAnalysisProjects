

Alzheimer’s Disease Detection using Machine Learning

Abstract 
The detection of Alzheimer's disease is a significant challenge in the medical field, and machine learning algorithms have shown promise in aiding early detection. In this study, we explore the use of machine learning algorithms for detecting Alzheimer's disease. We investigate the efficacy of various algorithms, including decision trees, support vector machines, and artificial neural networks, in detecting Alzheimer's disease using MRI brain images. We also examine the impact of different image pre-processing techniques on the performance of these algorithms. Our results show that machine learning algorithms can accurately detect Alzheimer's disease with high sensitivity and specificity, and that pre-processing techniques can significantly improve the accuracy of the algorithms. This study demonstrates the potential of machine learning algorithms in aiding the early detection of Alzheimer's disease, which can ultimately lead to more effective treatment and improved patient outcomes.

Introduction
Alzheimer's disease is a neurodegenerative disorder that affects millions of people worldwide, and early detection is crucial for better management of the disease. Machine learning algorithms have been gaining attention as a promising tool for Alzheimer's disease detection due to their ability to analyze large and complex datasets and identify patterns that are difficult for humans to detect.Neuroimaging data, such as magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) and positron emission tomography (PET) scans, can provide information about brain structure, function, and metabolism, which are useful for detecting Alzheimer's disease. However, analyzing neuroimaging data manually is a time-consuming and error-prone task, and the results can vary depending on the expertise of the person analyzing the data. Machine learning can overcome these limitations by automating the analysis process and providing more objective and accurate results.One of the most common approaches for Alzheimer's disease detection using machine learning is to extract features from neuroimaging data and then use classification algorithms to distinguish between Alzheimer's disease patients and healthy controls. Feature extraction involves transforming the raw neuroimaging data into a set of features that capture the most relevant information for the classification task. Different feature extraction techniques have been used, such as voxel-based morphometry (VBM), which measures the gray matter density in different brain regions, and cortical thickness, which measures the thickness of the cortical mantle. Other techniques include functional connectivity, which measures the synchronization of brain activity between different regions, and machine learning algorithms that can learn features directly from the raw imaging data, such as convolutional neural networks (CNNs).Once the features are extracted, various machine learning algorithms can be used for classification, such as support vector machines (SVMs), random forests, and artificial neural networks (ANNs). These algorithms learn from the features and use them to create a model that can predict whether a sample is from an Alzheimer's disease patient or a healthy control. The accuracy of the model depends on the quality of the features and the choice of the machine learning algorithm.Several studies have shown promising results in Alzheimer's disease detection using machine learning. For example, a study by Liu et al. (2014) used SVMs to classify Alzheimer's disease patients and healthy controls based on features extracted from MRI scans. The study achieved an accuracy of 87.5%, sensitivity of 91.3%, and specificity of 83.3%. Another study by Zhao et al. (2019) used a deep learning approach based on CNNs to classify Alzheimer's disease patients and healthy controls based on PET scans. The study achieved an accuracy of 91.4%, sensitivity of 88.4%, and specificity of 94.4%.Machine learning has also been used to predict the progression of Alzheimer's disease and to distinguish between different subtypes of the disease. A study by Zhang et al. (2018) used a random forest algorithm to predict the conversion of mild cognitive impairment (MCI) to Alzheimer's disease based on features extracted from MRI scans. The study achieved an accuracy of 81.0%, sensitivity of 75.8%, and specificity of 85.9%. Xie et al. (2020) used an ANN to classify Alzheimer's disease patients into different subtypes based on features extracted from PET scans. The study achieved an accuracy of 86.6%, sensitivity of 83.0%, and specificity of 91.1%.Despite the promising results, there are still challenges that need to be addressed before machine learning can be used in clinical practice for Alzheimer's disease detection. One challenge is the lack of standardization in neuroimaging data acquisition and processing, which can affect the performance of machine learning algorithms. Another challenge is the need for large and diverse datasets to train and validate the machine learning models. 

Related work

Morshedul et al. (2021) employed the Open Access Series of Imaging Studies (OASIS) dataset to investigate dementia prediction using various machine learning models. Despite the limited size of the dataset, it exhibits notable characteristics. The dataset has been thoroughly analyzed and has served as a benchmark for multiple machine learning studies. The models employed in the investigation include support vector machines, logistic regression, decision trees, and random forests, with and without fine-tuning. Results show that the support vector machine outperforms all other models, exhibiting the highest accuracy in detecting dementia among numerous patients.
Shahbaz et al. (2019) conducted a study that utilized the Alzheimer's Disease Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI) dataset to classify five different stages of Alzheimer's disease (AD) using six machine learning and data mining algorithms. The algorithms included k-nearest neighbor (k-NN), decision trees (DT), rule induction, Naive Bayes, generalized linear models (GLM), and deep learning algorithms. The study aimed to determine the most distinctive attribute for each stage of AD among the ADNI dataset. The study's findings revealed that GLM had the highest precision in categorizing the stages of AD with an accuracy of 88.24% on the test dataset. The results also suggested that these methods can be successfully applied in healthcare and medicine for early identification and detection of AD.
 Rallabandi,et al.(2020) introduced an automated method for classifying individuals with cognitively normal status, mild cognitive impairment, and Alzheimer's disease using structural MRI analysis. The proposed method employed various machine learning techniques and feature selection methods to accurately classify patients based on their MRI data. The study showed promising results, achieving high accuracy rates in classifying individuals with different cognitive statuses, which may have significant implications for the early detection and treatment of cognitive impairments.
Hamdi et al. (2022) developed an improved CAD system based on a convolutional neural network (CNN) to differentiate individuals with normal cognitive function from those with Alzheimer's disease. The system was tested using 18FDG-PET scans from 855 patients, including 220 Alzheimer's patients and 220 normal controls from the ADNI database. The results showed that the proposed CAD system outperformed existing literature-based approaches, achieving an accuracy of 96%, a sensitivity of 96%, and a specificity of 94%, indicating excellent performance.
Nawaz et al. (2021) propose an Alzheimer's stage detection method based on deep features extracted from a convolutional neural network (CNN) by transferring the initial layers from a pre-trained AlexNet model. To categorize the retrieved deep features, popular machine learning techniques such as support vector machine (SVM), k-nearest neighbour (KNN), and random forest (RF) are employed. The evaluation findings of the suggested scheme demonstrate that the deep feature-based model outperforms both handmade and deep learning methods, achieving a remarkable accuracy of 99.21%. Furthermore, the proposed model exhibits superior performance compared to current cutting-edge approaches. The study highlights the potential of combining deep learning and machine learning methods for early-stage detection of Alzheimer's disease.
Taeho, et al. ( 2019) aimed to classify Alzheimer's Disease (AD) and predict the transition from Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) to AD by utilizing various machine learning techniques. The study found that combining conventional machine learning with stacked auto-encoder (SAE) for feature selection yielded accuracy rates up to 98.8% for AD classification and 83.7% for MCI to AD conversion prediction. Furthermore, deep learning algorithms like Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) and Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN) without pre-processing achieved accuracy levels up to 96.0% for AD classification and 84.2% for MCI conversion prediction using neuroimaging data for feature selection. The best classification results were obtained by combining multimodal neuroimaging with fluid biomarkers. Overall, the research provides insights into the effectiveness of various machine learning techniques for the classification and prediction of AD and MCI conversion.
Altinkaya et al. utilized Artificial Neural Network (ANN) technology to detect Alzheimer’s disease at an early stage, with the aid of MRI (Magnetic Resonance Image) images. The study involved the classification of features extracted from brain MRI images, such as ventricular size, hippocampus shape, cortical thickness, and brain volume, for the purpose of AD detection. These features were further classified into groups, including mild cognitive impairment (MCI) and normal control (NC) groups. In addition to ANN, other deep learning techniques, such as Deep Neural Network (DNN), Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), Deep Automatic Encoder (DAE), and Deep Boltzmann Machine (DBM), were also employed for AD detection. The CNN model yielded the highest accuracy, at 99.9%, followed by the DNN model, at 99.2%.

Architecture 

Support Vector Machine (SVM) is a supervised learning algorithm used for classification and regression analysis. The SVM architecture consists of three main components: input layer, output layer, and kernel function.

Input Layer:
The input layer takes the input data and transforms it into a high-dimensional feature space. Each feature corresponds to a particular attribute of the input data, and each instance of the feature is a specific value of that attribute. For example, if we were classifying animals as either dogs or cats, the input layer might have features such as weight, height, fur length, and tail length.

Kernel Function:

The kernel function takes the transformed data from the input layer and computes the distance between the data points in the feature space. The kernel function maps the input data into a higher-dimensional space, where it is more likely to be linearly separable. The choice of kernel function is critical in SVM, as it determines how the input data is transformed and how well the SVM performs.Some common kernel functions used in SVM include:Linear kernel: This kernel function creates a linear boundary between the classes in the feature space. It is best suited for linearly separable data.Polynomial kernel: This kernel function creates a non-linear boundary between the classes in the feature space. It is best suited for data that has a polynomial distribution.Radial basis function (RBF) kernel: This kernel function creates a non-linear boundary between the classes in the feature space. It is best suited for data that has a non-linear distribution.Output Layer:The output layer takes the transformed data from the kernel function and outputs a prediction for each input instance. In classification problems, the output layer predicts the class of each input instance based on its location in the feature space. In regression problems, the output layer predicts a continuous value for each input instance.





We wrote a Python script that trains an SVM (Support Vector Machine) model on a dataset of brain MRI images to classify them as either non-demented or having one of three stages of Alzheimer's disease (very mild, mild, or moderate). The script uses OpenCV for image processing, scikit-learn for model training and evaluation, and scikit-image for feature extraction using Histogram of Oriented Gradients (HOG).

The script loads the images from a directory and preprocesses them by resizing, normalizing, and optionally performing histogram equalization and HOG feature extraction. It then splits the data into training and testing sets, performs SVM parameter tuning using GridSearchCV, trains an SVM classifier with the best parameters, and evaluates the classifier on the test set.

Finally, the script loads a separate test dataset and evaluates the model on that, printing the accuracy score, classification report, and confusion matrix. It also visualizes some of the test images with their predicted labels.

Overall, the script is a useful example of how to use machine learning to classify medical images and demonstrates the importance of proper image preprocessing and feature extraction in achieving good classification accuracy.









Results: 
In this study, we investigated the use of machine learning and Support Vector Machines (SVM) for the detection of Alzheimer's disease using MRI images. Our dataset consisted of MRI images from individuals across four categories: No, Very Mild, Mild, and Moderate.

We divided the dataset into four parts based on these categories and trained our SVM model using a radial basis function kernel. We also performed feature selection using a recursive feature elimination algorithm to identify the most relevant features for classification.

Our results showed that our SVM model was able to accurately classify MRI images into their respective categories with an overall accuracy of 90%. This accuracy rate is better than many other papers in the field of Alzheimer's disease detection using machine learning.

The rows 0, 1, 2, and 3 correspond to the four categories, with 0 representing No, 1 representing Very Mild, 2 representing Mild, and 3 representing Moderate. The columns in the table provide the following metrics:

Precision: This is the proportion of true positive classifications (i.e., MRI images correctly classified into a particular category) to the total number of positive classifications (i.e., the sum of true positives and false positives). In other words, it is a measure of how often the model correctly classified an image as belonging to a particular category.
Recall: This is the proportion of true positive classifications to the total number of actual positive cases (i.e., the sum of true positives and false negatives). In other words, it is a measure of how well the model was able to detect all the positive cases.
F1-score: This is the harmonic mean of precision and recall, which provides a balanced measure of both metrics. It is a useful metric for comparing models that have different precision-recall trade-offs.
Support: This is the number of MRI images in each category.
